When you build a data stack from scratch, you make decisions fast — often under pressure, often with incomplete information. Some of those decisions hold up for years. Others become the technical debt your team is still paying off two product cycles later.

Here's what I've learned from doing it more than once.

## Start With the Questions, Not the Tools

The most common mistake I've seen (and made) is leading with tooling. "We'll use Snowflake, dbt, Airflow, and Looker" before you've written down the three business questions the stack needs to answer.

The warehouse tier, the transformation approach, the BI layer — all of those are downstream of the questions. What grain does the business actually think in? Who owns the metrics definitions? How often does data need to be fresh? Start there.

## The dbt Layer Is Where You Buy Clarity

dbt is the right place to encode business logic. Not your ETL pipeline. Not your BI tool. Not a stored procedure that no one can find. dbt.

When business logic lives in dbt:
- It's version-controlled
- It's testable
- It's documented in the same place it's defined
- It's reviewable by someone other than the person who wrote it

The upfront investment is real. The payoff is that when a finance director asks "why did revenue drop in this model last Tuesday," you can actually answer.

## Invest in Your Staging Layer Early

The staging layer — one model per source table, light transformations, no business logic — feels like overhead when you're moving fast. It's not. It's the interface between your raw sources and your business models.

When you skip staging and write business logic directly against raw tables, you pay for it every time a source schema changes. The staging layer is where you absorb that cost gracefully.

## The Hardest Part Is People, Not Pipelines

The technical decisions matter. But the thing that determines whether a data stack succeeds is adoption. Are stakeholders using the dashboards? Do analysts trust the numbers? Is the team maintaining the models or working around them?

A technically perfect data warehouse that no one uses is worth exactly nothing. Invest in documentation, communication, and the relationships that make data trustworthy — not just the code that makes it run.
