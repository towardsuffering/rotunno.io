Every data warehouse forces you to make a choice: dimensional modeling with a star schema, or a flat denormalized wide table (sometimes called an OBT — One Big Table). Both patterns work. The right answer depends on your query patterns and who maintains the models downstream.

## What Star Schema Actually Buys You

The star schema separates your business entities (dimensions) from your business events (facts). A sale is a fact. The customer, product, date, and store are dimensions. The fact table stays narrow. The dimension tables carry descriptive attributes.

This buys you:

- **Grain clarity.** Every row in a fact table represents exactly one thing at exactly one level of detail.
- **Reusable dimensions.** `dim_customer` gets joined to revenue facts, support ticket facts, and subscription facts — one source of truth, many uses.
- **Query legibility.** A star schema query reads like business logic.

The downside: more models to maintain, more joins to write, and upfront design discipline required.

## What the Wide Table Buys You

A flat wide table pre-joins everything. One row per grain with every relevant attribute already attached. No joins at query time.

- **Speed.** Analytical tools perform better on wide tables for simple grain queries.
- **Lower overhead for non-engineers.** A dashboard user or data scientist can work with a wide table without knowing dimensional modeling.

The downside: duplication everywhere, changes cascade unpredictably, and the table becomes a dumping ground.

## The Practical Answer in dbt

In a modern dbt project, you don't choose once. The mart layer is where you decide per use case:

- Build star schemas for core entity-event relationships (orders, sessions, subscriptions)
- Build wide tables for your most common downstream consumers — dashboards, Python notebooks, ML feature stores

Document the decision. Future maintainers need to know *why*, not just *what*.
