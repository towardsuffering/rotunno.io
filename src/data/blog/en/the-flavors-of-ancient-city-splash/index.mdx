You built a clean model. You wrote solid tests. The dashboard looks great. And then you walk into the executive meeting and someone asks a question your chart can't answer — and the whole room loses confidence in the work.

Presenting data to non-technical stakeholders is a skill. Here's how I approach it.

## Lead With the Business Question, Not the Methodology

Your audience doesn't care how you cleaned the data. They care what it means for their decision. Start with the finding, not the process.

Instead of: "We built a model using the last 90 days of event data, applying a 7-day rolling window, and after normalizing for seasonality…"

Say: "Retention dropped 12 points in Q2 among customers who onboarded in March. Here's why that matters and what we recommend."

Get to the implication first. Methodology goes in the appendix.

## Design Dashboards for the Question, Not the Dataset

The most common dashboard mistake is displaying every metric the data makes available. A dashboard with 40 charts tells the user nothing because it tells them everything.

The question I ask before building: "What decision does this dashboard support?" If I can't answer that in one sentence, the dashboard isn't ready to be designed yet.

## Be Explicit About What You Don't Know

Stakeholders trust analysts who name their uncertainty. "This metric doesn't include returns, which means it overstates revenue by an estimated 8-12%" is a feature, not a bug. It shows rigor and prevents your number from being misused.

If you hedge without being specific, you just create anxiety. If you're specific about the limitation, you give people what they need to interpret the finding correctly.

## Know the Difference Between a Data Question and a Business Question

"Why did revenue drop?" is a business question. "Is there a data issue in the pipeline?" is a data question. Non-technical stakeholders are always asking business questions. Analytics engineers need to quickly determine whether the answer is in the data or in the code — and communicate that clearly.

When the answer is in the code, say so immediately. Don't let a data quality issue drag a business review off course for 20 minutes.

## Show the Trend, Not Just the Snapshot

A single number without context is almost always misleading. $2.4M in revenue means nothing without knowing whether that's up or down, expected or surprising, seasonal or structural.

The most useful thing you can add to almost any metric is: this month vs. last month, this month vs. same month last year, and whether the current value is inside or outside normal range. Three comparisons. That's it.
