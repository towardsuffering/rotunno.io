Walk into almost any company's BI tool and you'll find dashboards full of metrics: page views, conversion rate, revenue, churn, NPS, DAU, MRR, pipeline coverage. The metrics are real. The problem is that most of them exist because someone thought they should be tracked — not because they drive a specific decision.

Here's how I think about KPI design.

## A Metric Without a Decision Is Decoration

The test for any KPI is simple: "If this number goes up, what do we do differently? If it goes down, what do we do differently?"

If the answer to both questions is "we discuss it," the metric is not a KPI. It's wallpaper. Wallpaper dashboards are expensive to build, expensive to maintain, and generate noise without generating action.

Real KPIs are connected to specific decisions that specific people make on a specific cadence. The metric changes, someone acts.

## Vanity Metrics and Why They Spread

Vanity metrics — page views, total users, total revenue without segmentation — feel good because they tend to go up. They're easy to track and easy to share in all-hands meetings. They're hard to act on because they aggregate away the information you need.

Total revenue going up tells you nothing about whether the business is healthy. Revenue by segment, by cohort, by acquisition channel, by product line — that's where the signal lives. Vanity metrics optimize for looking good. Good KPIs optimize for making decisions.

## The Four Questions I Ask Before Designing Any KPI

**1. What decision does this metric support?**
Name the decision. Name the person who makes it. Name the cadence. If you can't, the metric isn't ready.

**2. Is this metric actionable at the grain we're measuring?**
A metric that's meaningful at the company level may be meaningless at the team level, and vice versa. "Churn rate" is useful for the CEO. "Churn rate by onboarding cohort by CSM" is useful for the CS team. Design for the decision-maker's grain.

**3. How does this metric fail?**
Every metric can be gamed or misread. A metric that only goes up is usually measuring the wrong thing. Know the failure mode before you ship the dashboard.

**4. Who owns this metric?**
Unowned metrics drift. Someone needs to be accountable for the definition, the freshness, and the accuracy. If no one owns it, don't build it.

## Leading vs. Lagging Indicators

Most business metrics are lagging — they tell you what happened. Revenue, churn, NPS. These are important but they describe the past.

Leading indicators predict what's about to happen. Trial starts predict revenue. Feature adoption predicts retention. Login frequency predicts engagement. The most useful dashboards combine both — here's what happened and here's what's coming.

The hard part: leading indicators require more analytical work to validate. You need to prove the leading-lagging relationship holds. That's the job — and it's where analytics engineers add disproportionate value.
