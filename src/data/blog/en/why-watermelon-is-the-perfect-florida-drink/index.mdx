Most analysts know SQL. Analytics engineers know *when* SQL is the right tool and *which* patterns make the difference between a query that works and a model that scales. Here are five patterns I use regularly in production data work.

## 1. The Spine Join

A spine is a complete list of every combination of grain you care about — every customer, every date, every product — whether or not there's event data for that combination.

Without a spine, your aggregation silently drops zeros. A customer who churned and had zero purchases last month disappears from your cohort analysis. A product with no sales doesn't appear in your inventory report.

The spine pattern: build a cross join of your dimensions, then left join your fact data to it, coalescing nulls to zero. Your reports become truthful about absence, not just presence.

## 2. LAG/LEAD for Sessionization and State Changes

Window functions with LAG and LEAD let you compare a row to its predecessor or successor in a partition. This is how you detect:
- The start and end of a user session
- State transitions (free → paid, active → churned)
- Gaps in expected time series data

The pattern: partition by entity, order by time, use LAG to get the previous value, then flag rows where the current value differs from the previous one.

## 3. Incremental Models With Idempotent Logic

Full refreshes are fine at small scale. At production scale, you need incremental models — only processing new or changed data. The key constraint: incremental logic must be idempotent, meaning you can run it twice and get the same result.

The pattern: filter on a watermark (usually `updated_at` or `event_date`), process the delta, and upsert on a unique key. In dbt, this means choosing the right `unique_key` and `incremental_strategy` for your warehouse.

## 4. The Metrics Layer With Consistent Filters

Inconsistent filters are where metric disagreements live. Revenue to finance includes refunds. Revenue to sales excludes refunds. Revenue to the CEO is "it depends."

The pattern: define your metric once with explicit filter documentation, and enforce that definition in a metrics layer model — not at the BI tool level. When the definition changes, it changes in one place.

## 5. Date Spining for Complete Time Series

When you're building time series reports, you need a row for every date in your range — including dates with no events. A date spine cross-joined to your entity list gives you that.

In dbt, `dbt-utils` provides `date_spine()`. The pattern: generate your spine, left join your aggregated events, coalesce metrics to 0, and your time series is complete. No more missing bars in your bar charts.
